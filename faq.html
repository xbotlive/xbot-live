Getting Started
A grid bot places a ladder of limit buy and sell orders at fixed price intervals within a defined range. When price drops, a buy fills. When it recovers, the corresponding sell fills. Each completed cycle — one buy and one sell at adjacent levels — captures the spread between them as profit. The bot repeats this automatically as long as price oscillates within the range.

ADX (Average Directional Index) below 20 indicates ranging conditions — the simulator's Market Analysis panel calculates this from live data when you select a market. High-volume, liquid perpetual futures pairs (BTC, ETH, SOL) are the most commonly used. Avoid low-liquidity markets where spreads and slippage will erode returns.

Using the Simulator
Monte Carlo generates hundreds of synthetic price paths using your IV (implied volatility) and drift assumptions. It shows you a probability distribution of outcomes — what could happen across a wide range of scenarios. Backtest mode uses real historical OHLCV data that you upload as a CSV. It shows you exactly what would have happened on a specific market during a specific period. Use Monte Carlo for scenario planning; use backtesting for validation against real price history.
The most common sources are Binance (via their data portal at data.binance.vision), TradingView (export from the chart), Bybit, and CoinGecko. Download as CSV in standard OHLCV format (timestamp, open, high, low, close, volume). The simulator auto-detects column order and timestamp format. Aim for at least 90 days of data at 1-hour or 4-hour intervals for a meaningful backtest.
The liquidation price is calculated from your average entry price, which depends on where your opening position is placed. For a long grid bot, the opening position fills at the entry price going down — so a wider grid range that extends lower changes the average entry, which shifts the liquidation price. Adjusting leverage has the most direct impact on liquidation distance.

If the seed were different, outcome differences could be caused by random variation rather than the strategy difference you're testing.

Grid Mechanics
The profit earned from one complete cycle: a buy order fills, then the corresponding sell order fills at the next level up. For an arithmetic grid, this equals the grid spacing minus the round-trip maker fee cost. The simulator displays this as a dollar amount and a percentage of order size. Break-even oscillations = opening taker cost ÷ profit per round trip.
Arithmetic spacing places grid levels at equal dollar intervals (e.g. every $500). Geometric spacing places them at equal percentage intervals (e.g. every 0.5%). For tight ranges, arithmetic is simpler. For wide ranges — especially above $10,000 in price — geometric is more appropriate because the same percentage move at $90,000 is a much larger dollar move than at $60,000. Most exchange grid bots default to geometric.
A neutral grid runs two independent order books simultaneously — a long book (buy orders below entry) and a short book (sell orders above entry). As price falls, buys fill and open long positions. As price rises, sells fill and open short positions. At entry price, the net position is flat. Neutral grids earn from oscillation without taking a directional bias, but they are more complex: both books have independent liquidation prices and funding exposure.
If price trends strongly in one direction and stays there, the bot accumulates a growing open position in the opposite direction. For a long bot in a falling market, this means a large and increasingly underwater long position. The bot continues placing orders but cannot close them until price recovers. This is the primary risk. The TP/SL settings allow you to cap the loss by closing the position if price moves too far against you.
Risk & Funding
Perpetual futures use a funding mechanism to keep the perpetual price close to the spot price. Every 8 hours (or 1 hour on some exchanges), long positions pay short positions (or vice versa) a small percentage of their notional value. At 0.01% per 8 hours, a $10,000 notional position costs $1 every 8 hours — $1,095 per year. For leveraged grid bots running large notional positions, funding is a significant drag. The simulator models this continuously.
Liq price = avg entry × (1 − 1/leverage + MMR + 2×taker_fee + funding_per_period) for a long position. The key inputs are leverage (lower leverage = further liquidation), maintenance margin rate (exchange-specific, typically 0.5%), and average entry price (which depends on how many grid levels have filled). The simulator shows both a static display estimate and uses a dynamic formula during simulation that accounts for realised P&L shifting as the bot earns.
Yes, in two scenarios. First, funding drag: if funding rates are high and the simulation runs long enough, cumulative funding payments can exceed grid income. The simulator shows median funding paid alongside median grid P&L so you can see this directly. Second, a neutral grid can accumulate unrealised losses on one book if price drifts to one side of the range and stays there, even without liquidation.
For Long and Short bots, opening the initial position costs a taker fee (because market orders are used to open). Break-even oscillations = that opening cost ÷ profit per round trip. If break-even is 12 oscillations and your simulation shows a median of 40 oscillations, you have significant margin before the bot becomes unprofitable purely from a fee perspective. Neutral grids do not have an opening cost (all orders are placed as resting limits), so break-even does not apply.
Results Interpretation
The percentage of simulated price paths where the bot ends with positive total P&L (grid income + unrealised position − funding paid). A win rate of 65% means 65% of the 500 simulated scenarios ended in profit. It is not the percentage of individual grid orders that filled profitably — every completed round trip is profitable by construction.
Annualised return divided by annualised standard deviation of daily returns, calculated across all simulated paths. A Sharpe above 1.0 indicates good risk-adjusted returns. Negative Sharpe means the strategy lost money on average. Sharpe is calculated from daily P&L variation across all paths in the simulation, not from a single path.
Annualised median return divided by median maximum drawdown. Higher is better. A Calmar of 2.0 means the strategy returned twice its typical worst peak-to-trough loss on an annualised basis. It is a more intuitive risk measure than Sharpe for strategies with skewed return distributions, which grid bots often have.
The P95 outcome is the 95th percentile — better than 95% of simulated paths. It represents a fortunate scenario where price oscillated frequently within the range and never came close to the grid boundaries. The median is a more reliable planning figure. The gap between median and P95 reflects the range of possible outcomes — a wide gap means high variance in results.
The amber line is the median P&L path across all simulations. The blue shaded band is the 10th–90th percentile range — 80% of simulated paths fall within it. The outer band (lighter) covers the 25th–75th percentile range. When price paths are shown, the green line is the best outcome (highest final price), the red line is the worst, and the amber line is the median.
